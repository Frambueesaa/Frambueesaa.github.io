First time with C++ in Unreal Engine 5

From Unity to Unreal Engine, C# and its father C++.

Updated on June 6th, 2025 at 2:23 PM.

I had been eyeing Unreal Engine for a while now. You might think, like most people, that it's because of the idea of creating a AAA game or at least simulating its graphics, but no — if there's something developers love, it's challenges, and when it comes to code, I start overthinking... probably more than I should. All of this, combined with some questionable policies from Unity, my main game engine, and my constant interest in C++, with its manual memory management — also encouraged by opinions from some programmers I met at game dev events — led me to dive into this world.

Easy to start?

I’ve only been using C++ for a few weeks and made just a few attempts with the engine and Blueprints. From my experience with Unity, I can say it’s not easy. It's a very complete engine, no doubt about that, and I haven’t even scratched the surface of what it offers — but it’s extremely complex, especially for someone just starting out, at least with C++. From the Blueprints perspective, it’s a bit more accessible. In fact, for 3D artists and game designers, the engine is exactly what they need, since it offers a wide variety of tools that Unity, at least natively, doesn’t include.

What about C++?

If you're coming from native C++, it’ll probably be easier for you than it was for me. But it’s true that Unreal Engine is a massive engine, so you'll need to learn a lot first about how it works and how to implement things. This isn’t like Unity where you just create and drag — here, every class is strictly tied to an object or a blueprint, or at least that’s what it seemed like to me in this brief first contact.

Will I continue with Unreal Engine?

Obviously yes. The engine is something that really catches my attention, and the visual results it delivers so easily are incredible. I also love the idea Unreal maintains of combining Blueprints with native C++ code. This way, when collaborating with another developer with less experience, they can still contribute to the project easily. Plus, it’s great for rapid prototyping.
